The Principality of Andorra reaffirms and strengthens its commitment to action global climate by submitting its Third Determined Contribution to National level (NDC 3.0), aligned with the principles and objectives established in the Paris Agreement. This international framework, adopted in 2015, constitutes the roadmap to address the climate crisis, with the aim of limiting the increase in the average global temperature to 2°C above the pre-industrial level and strive not to exceed 1.5°C.

Andorra is aligned not only with the goals of the Paris Agreement, but also with the commitments arising from the First Global Stocktake presented in 2023 at the Conference of the Parties held in Dubai (COP 28). This stocktake highlights the urgent need to intensify collective efforts to close the gap between current emissions and the goal of limiting global temperature rise and highlights the importance of countries adopting more ambitious goals and inclusive approaches that combine mitigation, adaptation and climate finance.

This first assessment highlights the urgent need to intensify efforts and urges Parties to submit new NDCs between 9 and 12 months before COP30/CMA7, i.e. by 10 February 2025. It also urges Parties to that the new NDCs represent progress compared to previous ones and establish new emission reduction commitments that reflect the highest level of ambition possible by 2035.

This NDC also takes into account key findings from the Sixth Report of the Assessment of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change1

In terms of adaptation to climate change, Andorra has considered the provisions planned by the Global Adaptation Goal that seek to improve resilience to to climate change in key areas such as water, food production, health systems, ecosystems, infrastructure, poverty and heritage cultural. This Global Goal also sets out key considerations for meeting their targets. By 2027, all Parties should have implemented multi-hazard early warning systems, climate information services for risk reduction, and systematic observations. By 2030, all Parties are expected to have established policy instruments, processes, or planning strategies, including National Adaptation Plans, and to have made progress in their implementation. This should contribute to reducing the social and economic impacts of major climate shocks.

3. Andorra’s commitment

Andorra's historical responsibility in terms of contributing to climate change is limited due to its size, small population and very low level of industrialization. However , Andorra assumes its commitment and responsibility to global climate action by aligning its emission reduction goals with international objectives. The country takes a further step in terms of transparency and commitment, moving from a relative reduction to its Business as Usual (BAU) scenario to an absolute reduction relative to its peak emissions.

emissions reached in 2005.

Likewise, Andorra establishes a new climate goal even more ambitious by 2035, including a 63% reduction in its net GHG emissions compared to 2005 levels. According to the latest GHG emissions inventory presented by Andorra on its BTR 1, the emission levelsNet emissions in 2005 were 370.73 Gg CO2eq (AR5, GWP). The target for 2035 represents in absolute terms emissions of 137 Gg CO2eq.

This effort is part of a path consistent with the 1.5°C limit and the commitment to achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, reaffirming the country's leadership in climate action and its commitment to the principles of the UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement.

This target applies to the entire economy, includes all sectors and categories of anthropogenic emissions and removals according to the National GHG Emissions Inventory , and all GHGs considered in the 2006 IPCC Guidelines. As mentioned above, Andorra complies punctually with its reporting obligations under the UNFCCC and therefore updates its GHG emissions inventory biennially. With each update,

Improves inventory quality and recalculates time series as needed to reflect findings most recent and maintain methodological consistency as as established by the IPCC Guidelines.

This new NDC represents a change in the typology of the goal. that Andorra had communicated until now in its previous NDCs. So far, the goals communicated by Andorra have been business as usual reference scenario

BAU). In this new NDC, the country adopts a target of absolute reduction of emissions in relation to the levels of its peak emissions year, which in the case of Andorra corresponds to 2005.

a) Energy production

With the approval of Law 21/2018, of September 13, to promote the energy transition and climate change (Litecc)56

In this context, the most significant project currently being promoted by the Andorran Government is the construction of the country's first wind farm. This infrastructure will mark a milestone in the country's energy transition, enabling national production to increase from the current 20% to more than 30% of Andorra's energy demand, directly contributing to the national objectives set by Law 21/2018 (Litecc). The supporting Sector Plan contemplates the installation of 10 wind turbines, which will generate an estimated annual production of 51 GWh, equivalent to the energy consumption of about 15,000 homes.

In addition to increasing national energy production renewable, the wind farm will strengthen the energy sovereignty of the country, significantly increasing the country’s resilience to the global energy crises, and exposure to fluctuations in energy prices in neighboring countries.

Along the same lines, but on a smaller scale, the Renova program of Aid for the rehabilitation of buildings also includes subsidies for self-consumption and injection installations, primarily through photovoltaic systems. Thanks to this program, more than €3 million has been awarded since 2018, subsidizing more than 500 photovoltaic installations, representing more than 7.5 MW of installed capacity. This represents an important contribution to reducing dependence on fossil fuels, reducing emissions, improving the country's energy security, and decentralizing generation.

b) Exemplary role of the Administration

The Public Administration has an exemplary role in the creation of a new energy consumption model. Thus, since the publication of the Decree 365/20228, the exemplary role of the administration has been focused on providing the Government with effective tools to implement Energy-saving measures, laying the foundation for a more efficient, sustainable, and economical energy model. In this context, the main actions taken are as follows:

  • Energy renovation of government buildings. Based on the energy audits conducted, priority has been given to those buildings that require the most urgent renovation from a purely energy perspective, in order to meet the required energy efficiency requirements (energy rating A).

Thus, a budget has been allocated for the energy rehabilitation of priority properties.

  • Installation of solar panels on all buildings of the Administration, to optimize efficiency economic and avoid costs to public finances. provides that these facilities will be managed by granting spaces on the roofs from buildings to energy service companies. These companies will assume the initial investment, recovering it through the sale of electricity generated, and once amortized, the facilities will become the property of the Government.
  • Certification of the renewable origin of the electricity consumed by the General Administration, through the national origin guarantee system.
  • Promoting energy monitoring in public buildings. Energy consumption monitoring in General Administration buildings will be implemented progressively until 2028 in blocks of six buildings per year, covering all 36 government buildings, including 12 schools already part of the project. This system will provide real-time information.

to plan and evaluate the effectiveness of the measures implemented.

  • Progressive electrification of the public vehicle fleet, reaching 30% of electric vehicles in 2030 and a 70% in 2050. This includes the minimum incorporation of 7 annual electric vehicles until 2035, and the rationalization of the fleet by reducing the 1% of the annual number of vehicles, which is equivalent to 35 fewer vehicles by 2050.
  • Information to the user about the energy rating of public buildings to raise awareness on energy consumption.
  • Collaboration agreement with administrations premises to promote the rationalization of energy consumption and optimize the use of energy resources.

c) Decarbonization of the building sector

Law 21/2018 (Litecc) provides for specific actions to promote the energy saving and energy efficiency in buildings. actions in this area can be divided into: rehabilitation of the existing buildings, reducing energy dependence on to hydrocarbons (for heat production in the energy sector) building), efficient energy management and regulation of criteria for the construction of new buildings under nearly zero-energy building (NZEB) criteria.

To this end, the Government has implemented regulations with advanced energy requirements to ensure that newly built homes meet the nearly zero-energy consumption standard and incorporate a minimum percentage of on-site energy production from renewable sources. Furthermore, the regulations are expected to be amended to require that the resulting planning study the possibilities of implementing centralized heat networks in the units of action, in the pre-construction phase of building projects.

The Renova program incentivizes building renovation through direct subsidies and loans with a direct state guarantee to implement improvements such as window replacement, improved facade insulation, and the installation of efficient thermal systems. This program directly contributes to achieving of the objective of reducing national energy intensity by 20% by 2030 and the goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 40% greenhouse gas emissions in the building sector by 2030 compared to the year 2017.

Throughout the various annual calls dating back to 2011, the Renova program has become the instrument main to develop a good part of the policy of improvement of the efficiency and energy savings in buildings. This is demonstrated by the percentages of acceptance of the concepts of energy efficiency and implementation of renewable energy in the latest calls, which have exceeded 80% of the total applications.

From 2011 to 2023, nearly 2,500 grants have been awarded for energy renovation or energy improvements (Chapters 4, 5, and 8 of the calls), totaling more than €8.5 million. This represents the total or partial renovation of 5.27% of the country's total properties (considering that, in 2022, there were a total of 46,790 properties).

 

d) Sustainable mobility

Since 2022, public transport is free in Andorra. This measure seeks to encourage its use and reduce the number of trips made in private vehicles, aligning with the goal of reducing emissions from internal mobility by 50% by 2030. It is an inclusive measure that benefits the entire population and has the potential to transform mobility habits, significantly reducing transport emissions.

Thus, the use of public bus services has tripled since September 2019 and doubled since the implementation of free service in July 2022. This increase confirms the consolidation of public transportation as a preferred option among citizens, reflecting its growing acceptance and usefulness.

In addition to the free nature of the service, significant improvements have been introduced in recent years that reinforce its accessibility and efficiency. These actions include line optimization Andorra-La Seu d'Urgell, which connects the country with the neighboring territory where more than 1,800 cross-border workers live (in 2023), and the extension of night bus schedules, facilitating mobility during non-conventional hours and responding to the group of workers linked to the services sector.

On the other hand, the Engega aid program has been in operation since 2014 promoting the acquisition of electric or hybrid vehicles plug-in. The Government of Andorra has helped finance more than 1,681 electric, hybrid or low-emission vehicles along the route of these years.

 

e) National forest inventory

To achieve carbon neutrality by 2050, it is essential Preserve the country's main carbon sink: its forests. Thanks to these forests, approximately 40% of the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions attributable to Andorra (according to IPCC guidelines) are absorbed, underscoring its crucial role in mitigating climate change.

These data have recently been updated thanks to the first National Forest Inventory of Andorra (INF)9Andorra Recerca + Innovació. This inventory is a key tool for understanding in detail the state, extent, and capacity of the country's forests to act as carbon sinks. Furthermore, the inventory Forestry will be updated periodically every 10 years, providing real data on the evolution of the capacity of carbon absorption of the country and allowing strategies to be adjusted management and conservation based on the needs detected, with the aim of enhancing its sink capacity.

More recently, to ensure that adaptation measures are consistent and adapt to real and expected changes in the territory, efforts and resources continue to be dedicated to the Study of the impact and vulnerability in relation to climate change

(EVICC), which characterizes the key sectors for adaptation in Andorra: energy, tourism, health and agriculture.

Based on the prioritized sectors, the 94 adaptation measures identified by the PAACC in 2014 will be reviewed collaboratively with the Andorra Recerca + Innovació research center , as well as for improving knowledge of the sink capacity with the preparation of the first National Forest Inventory (INF). Actions resulting from the 2014 process include strategies for planning, operational, regulatory development, etc. and include very broad areas, not exclusively related to sectors socioeconomic, such as landscape and biodiversity.

It is expected to complete the necessary studies to update the measures adaptation of the priority areas in Law 21/2018 (Litecc) before 2027, and with them update the actions and propose a more up-to-date adaptation roadmap that includes the latest information and existing technological advances. b) Adaptation at the regional level

Andorra is located entirely in mountainous terrain and is, in turn, in such a small country, cooperation is of great interest. regional administrations and research centers, especially from the countries and regions that coexist in the bioregion of the Pyrenees mountain range.

In this regard, Andorra, within the framework of cross-border cooperation at the Pyrenees level, participates in the Pyrenees Working Community Consortium (CTP), which includes the Principality of Andorra, the Spanish autonomous communities of Aragon, Catalonia, the Basque Country and Navarre, and the French regions of Nouvelle-Aquitaine and Occitanie, and more specifically, since 2010, in the Pyrenean Climate Change Observatory (OPCC), which aims to monitor and understand the phenomenon of climate change in the Pyrenees, while also ensuring the knowledge necessary for the implementation of effective and common adaptation measures.

In 2022, this cross-border working group developed the Pyrenees Cross-border Climate Change Adaptation Strategy, one of the objectives within the framework of the ADAPYR project, and in 2023 obtained the support of a Life-SIP program (PYRENEES4CLIMA) to develop the operational plan of this strategy in the territory (an 8-year project)10

In this sense, the LIFE PYRENEES4CLIMA project will support the implementation of the first European climate change strategy for a mountain and transboundary bioregion (EPiCC). The 7 CTP member territories have committed to cooperate to the implementation of the strategy and are an active part of this proposal also acting as partners in an individual capacity. The main objective of the EPiCC strategy is to propose actions that cover both the adaptation as, synergistically with that, mitigation, aimed at radically improving the resilience of the Spanish-French-Andorran Pyrenees region to climate change, and the project LIFE SIP Integrated supports its full implementation, and provides greater stability to the mission entrusted to the Pyrenean Observatory of Change CTP Climate.

One of the expected outcomes at the end of this Life-SIP project (2031) is the development of a climate services platform with real-time information on climate risks (drought and heat waves). This platform, with limited administrative and resource capacities due to its size, such as Andorra's, could be of great value for achieving the objectives defined in the Global Goal on Adaptation.

In this sense, another of the experiences for the entire bioregion is to analyze ecological connectivity and the fragmentation of natural spaces. The objective is to identify discontinuities. Ecological assessments at the massif scale regarding the habitats most vulnerable to climate change are being conducted to propose preservation and restoration measures. Precise joint mapping is currently being developed to later determine priority areas for action.

Also, in the area of improving the resilience of natural spaces, another project in Andorra will evaluate future national water resources by combining water management models, seasonal forecasting, and climate scenarios.

c) International commitment on climate change

In its commitment to climate action and strengthening international cooperation, Andorra participates in various multilateral initiatives. Among these, its participation in the Ibero-American Network of Climate Change Agencies (RIOCC) and in the Francophonie group of countries stands out, actively participating in the events held annually within the framework of the COP. Through these spaces, experiences are exchanged and opportunities are offered to identify synergies, strengths, and priorities in terms of regional cooperation and support between countries.

At the same time, Andorra is working to raise the issues of mountainous regions to the global political agenda, positioning itself as a key player in the defense of these vulnerable territories, highlighting the crucial role they play in biodiversity and the provision of essential water resources for millions of people.

a. National circumstances, such as geography, climate, economy, sustainable development and poverty eradication;

Geography

Andorra is a mountainous country, located in the heart of the Pyrenees mountain range and nestled between Spain and France.

Covering an area of 467.72 km2 and with rugged terrain, Andorra has an average altitude of 2,044 meters above sea level (m). Its capital, Andorra la Vella, is the highest European capital (1,050 m). The highest areas are found in the northern half of the territory, with Coma Pedrosa peak as its highest point (2,942 m), and the lowest point (837 m) on the southern border with Spain.

Historically, the configuration of the relief and the presence of the most fertile lands irrigated by the main rivers favored the settlement of the most populated towns and villages in the valley floors. The country's development during the 20th century took place in these areas in particular, with a significant increase in urbanized areas. Agricultural activities are primarily located in the valley floors. After a period of deforestation in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, forests reclaimed land from abandoned meadows and prairies, and currently approximately 37% of the country's territory is covered by forest, while infrastructure and urban areas occupy only 2%.

In terms of ecosystems, Andorra is a country with rich biodiversity. Its position in the middle of the Pyrenees, with a large part of the territory located on the Mediterranean side, gives it a significant diversity of climatic conditions, encompassing both Mediterranean types and typical high-mountain alpine environments. The large altitudinal gradient provides the ideal conditions for a wide range of different habitats that support a large number of living beings, some of them unique or even endemic. Traditional farming and livestock farming have contributed to increasing the diversity and mosaic of habitats and biodiversity.


Climate

Located in the middle of the central zone of the alpine bioregion of the Pyrenees, the climate of Andorra is a mid-latitude humid mountain climate with climate characteristics

Continental Mediterranean climate, and a temperate oceanic climate in the north. Naturally, all of this is greatly influenced by altitude. Taking into account regional and local factors, Andorra's climate is mountainous Mediterranean with a clear subcontinental tendency.

The climate in Andorra is evolving rapidly. Based on average annual temperature and precipitation data obtained at two stations in the country for the study period 1950-2020, the average annual temperature has increased by +0.22°C/decade. If this evolution is analyzed for the last 50 years (1970-2020), the increase is even more significant, reaching +0.34°C/decade.

Specifically, the year 2023 was the warmest in Andorra since records began, and over the last three years, Andorra has already exceeded an anomaly of 1.5°C.

Another relevant fact is that tropical nights were a practically non-existent phenomenon in the middle of the 20th century and, in the last year, they have increased by almost 6 days in the whole of the Pyrenees.

Furthermore, mountain ecosystems have been identified by the IPCC as among the most vulnerable to climate change, as temperatures evolve most rapidly in these regions, both in terms of impacts on their populations and the ecosystem services they provide. These climate variations have serious consequences for socioeconomic activities, especially those closely related to biodiversity, landscape, and nature, placing mountain peoples and their livelihoods in an extremely vulnerable position.

Demography

Andorra is a small country in terms of area, but with 86,931 inhabitants (2024), it represents a much higher population density than the rest of the Pyrenees. This density is much higher if we consider only the urbanized area.

Andorra's population has grown exponentially since the mid-20th century. Economic development and the high immigration rates that occurred in the second half of the 20th century explain this increase and the variety of nationalities that make up Andorran society.


Economy

The Andorran economy is strongly focused on tertiary activities. Services are the most important sector.

An important sector of the Principality's economy, with more than 8,000 companies and over 80% of the workforce. The main activities in this sector are commerce, hotels and restaurants, as well as activities related to financial services.

Commerce is a very important element in Andorra's economy and one of the country's main tourist attractions, offering more competitive prices than neighboring countries, flexible hours, and a wide range of products. This sector attracts the majority of visitors for business reasons, but these visitors' motivations also include sightseeing in general, exploring and exploring Andorra, skiing, gastronomy, nature, cultural heritage, hydrotherapy (health and wellness), and spa treatments.

Tourism is one of the pillars of Andorra's economy, directly or indirectly responsible for more than 80% of the country's GVA. Tourism is increasingly diversified and attracts nearly 8 million visitors annually, although with a strong seasonality linked to winter activities, but with a good turnout during the summer months.

In winter, Andorra is considered a leading destination in the world of snow, with four ski areas offering more than 300 km of slopes: Grandvalira (the largest in the Pyrenees), Ordino-Arcalís, Pal-Arinsal , and Naturlàndia (a Nordic ski resort).

During the summer, ski areas diversify their offerings with activities such as mountain biking, golf, adventure trails, hiking, and family activities, among others. The country offers other outdoor activities beyond those already mentioned, such as hiking, via ferrata, canyoning, climbing, fishing, and ecotourism trails.


Sustainable development

Due to its territorial and geomorphological characteristics, Andorra faces significant challenges related to urban management and population growth. The limited available land area, combined with its mountainous terrain, requires careful planning that ensures a balance between socioeconomic development and the conservation of the natural environment. In this context, proper land management becomes an essential pillar for moving toward a sustainable development model.

To address these challenges, current legislation requires local governments (Comuns) to submit studies of parish maximum carrying capacity. These studies aim to assess the sustainable growth limit for each parish, considering factors such as land use, resource availability, existing infrastructure, and environmental impact.

Once completed, these studies must be submitted to the Government, which is responsible for issuing a mandatory and binding report. This report serves as a basis for the Comuns (Comuns) to adapt their parish urban development plans (POUPs) in line with the conclusions drawn from the studies. This aims to ensure that urban growth is planned coherently, respecting environmental constraints and optimizing the use of available resources.


Eradication of poverty

Commerce is a very important element in Andorra's economy and one of the country's main tourist attractions, offering more competitive prices than neighboring countries, flexible hours, and a wide range of products. This sector attracts the majority of visitors for business reasons, but these visitors' motivations also include sightseeing in general, exploring and exploring Andorra, skiing, gastronomy, nature, cultural heritage, hydrotherapy (health and wellness), and spa treatments.

The progressive rise in the cost of living, currently exacerbated by the repercussions of various global challenges, requires redoubling efforts in key areas such as the pension system, access to housing, and, to a lesser extent, the fight against energy insecurity. All the social assistance that makes up the country's social protection system and the measures adopted to facilitate access to it help cushion the direct consequences of these challenges, preventing a significant portion of the population from falling into poverty and social deprivation.

The housing crisis currently constitutes one of Andorra's greatest challenges, due to rising housing purchase and rental prices, which makes it difficult to access decent housing. The limited supply of affordable housing means that sustainability and energy efficiency criteria are taking a backseat when making choices, exacerbating the barriers to choosing comfortable housing or selecting properties based on energy-related criteria. In response to this problem, the government is implementing measures to put social or affordable rental apartments on the market and, at the same time, is working to increase the population's purchasing power through initiatives such as increasing the minimum wage and making public transportation free, which seek to alleviate the financial pressure faced by Andorran households.

Regarding energy insecurity, aid for vulnerable families has increased significantly, reaching 136 households in 2022 compared to 55 in 2016. This aid, integrated into the social protection system, has helped reduce the vulnerability of the most affected families, but the extent of the problem remains uncertain, as the number of households experiencing energy insecurity who do not apply for available aid is unknown. Thus, a more detailed analysis is needed to implement measures that reach the entire affected population.

Likewise, pension system reform, which guarantees decent and sustainable pensions over time, represents a priority issue for the public authorities, which are working to promote a State Pact for the sustainability of the pension system that will allow for the implementation of urgent measures to guarantee the system's longterm sustainability.
